Coaching Training
Academic Belonging
A research-grounded faculty development workshop co-designed and co-facilitated with Dr. Jennifer Roth-Burnette, adapted for UA's coaching staff. Guides coaches through 18 learning activities organized across all six levels of Bloom's Taxonomy, each designed to simultaneously promote academic belonging and higher-order thinking. One of the strongest artifact-supported and data-supported projects in the portfolio.
Co-Design & Co-Facilitation Research Synthesis Bloom's Taxonomy Pre/Post Assessment Differentiated Instruction Content Adaptation
1
The Need
Coaches embedded in classrooms without tools for building belonging or driving higher-order thinking

Post-pandemic disruption had intensified pre-existing gaps in student belonging and engagement across UA courses. Coaches embedded in those classrooms needed the same evidence-based toolkit as faculty, without it, their in-class role was limited to passive presence rather than active support for the cognitive and belonging conditions that retention research identifies as critical.

2
The Learner
Coaches and faculty across disciplines, experience levels, and course sizes

Originally delivered to College of Arts and Sciences faculty spanning disciplines from STEM to humanities, then adapted for coaching staff who were embedded in those same courses. The design was built to scale across small (10 to 60), medium (61 to 120), and large (121+) class formats so every participant could apply the activities in their actual context.

3
The Approach
18 activities across all six Bloom's levels, grounded in four peer-reviewed sources

Grounded in a literature review drawing on Strayhorn, Hogan and Sathy, Pichon, and Hurney et al. on academic belonging and retention. Used Bloom's Taxonomy as the organizing framework, mapping 18 learning activities across all six cognitive levels, with three activity options at each level scaled by class size. Used the "Live the Lesson" structure throughout: coaches and faculty experienced each activity type as a learner before being asked to apply it. Collected pre/post self-assessment data to measure attitude and behavior change.

Results
91%
reported creating a stronger focus on student belonging post-workshop
81%
said the activities were simple to incorporate into their curriculum
26.4%
identified Evaluate as the easiest Bloom's level for promoting belonging
22.5%
identified Apply as second most accessible for belonging work

Pre/post self-assessment data showed increases across all three measured behaviors: considering student belonging while planning courses, using Bloom's Taxonomy to plan activities, and prioritizing peer-to-peer learning collaboration.
