Coaching Training
Time Management & Organization
A professional development workshop for UA coaching staff teaching time management as a five-step professional competency: knowing what is on your plate, goal setting, task prioritization, scaling organization across time horizons, and maintaining momentum. Coaches both learned the system as a professional tool and were trained to deliver it to students as part of their broader coaching responsibilities.
SMART Goals Prioritization Frameworks Train-the-Trainer Job Aid Design Multi-Format Resource Suite
1
The Need
Growing coach responsibilities outpacing informal time management habits

As the coaching program grew and coaches took on increasingly complex responsibilities, including managing their own caseloads, scheduling sessions, running programming, and onboarding new team members, time management became a critical professional competency rather than an assumed one. Without a shared, evidence-based framework, productivity varied across the team and depended too heavily on individual habit rather than program infrastructure.

2
The Learner
Coaches at all stages of the program, from newly hired to expanding into leadership

Professional coaching staff and peer coaches ranging from newly hired coaches encountering the full scope of the role for the first time to returning coaches taking on expanded responsibilities as the program grew. Both groups needed a replicable, scalable system that could travel with them as their roles evolved.

3
The Approach
A replicable five-step framework, deployable at both personal and coaching levels

Structured around Know Your Plate, Goal Setting, Prioritization, Organization, and Maintenance, using SMART Goals and an Eisenhower-style Action Priority Matrix as core frameworks. Scaled organization tools across four time horizons (quarterly, monthly, weekly, daily) with QR-linked digital resources. Coaches completed guided in-session practice building their own time management plan, with a second layer of design intent made explicit: the same framework and resource suite could be facilitated directly with students in coaching sessions.

Results

Participant feedback noted that the session replaced a fixed belief about being "bad at time management" with a concrete set of actionable professional steps. The framework was subsequently deployed across the coaching team's student-facing sessions as part of the retention curriculum.
