Retention Programs
Skill Sessions Curriculum
A student-facing academic skill-building curriculum deployed across all four retention programs at UA. Designed to equip students with practical, evidence-based strategies for note taking and information intake, and advanced study skills grounded in cognitive science, addressing the academic performance gaps driving retention risk in the first place.
Retention Curriculum Design Cognitive Learning Science Bloom's Taxonomy Multi-Method Instruction Stakeholder Partnership Pre/Post Data Collection
1
The Need
Academic performance gaps underlying retention risk across all four programs

Students in all four retention programs shared a common root cause beneath their GPA and credit-hour challenges: passive, inefficient academic habits that could not support the rigor of college coursework. Improving retention required addressing those habits directly, not just tracking them.

2
The Learner
Students across four retention populations, from scholarship risk to GPA recovery

Undergraduate students ranging from scholarship recipients at continuation risk (Uplift) to freshmen below a 2.0 GPA (Tide Watch) to general coaching participants. Most had never been explicitly taught structured academic strategies and were relying on passive or underdeveloped habits without awareness that evidence-based alternatives existed.

3
The Approach
Two curriculum modules deployed across all programs, designed for live practice against real material

Note Taking and Information Intake taught reading via SQ5R, four note-taking formats (Outline, Cornell, Charts, Mind Map), and study-tool creation, using sourced and edited WIRED/Brian Greene physics clips as intentionally complex practice material. Advanced Study Skills taught five cognitive science-backed strategies (Retrieval Practice, Interleaving, Spaced Repetition, Dual Coding, Self-Explanation) organized around Bloom's Taxonomy, with a dedicated engineering-student version built through a College of Engineering faculty partnership.

Results

A 6-week follow-up on Note Taking showed sustained multi-method adoption across all five strategies. A College of Engineering faculty partner noted the Advanced Study Skills curriculum gave students the tools needed to tackle complex engineering concepts and enhanced comprehension and retention across the college.
