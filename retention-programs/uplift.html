Retention Programs
Uplift
A structured retention program for roughly 250 scholarship students at the University of Alabama who were on academic probation and at risk of losing their funding. Uplift combined a four-week Skill Session onboarding sequence with ongoing monthly coaching, built two distinct support tiers for a population with divergent needs, and required coordination across a high-stakes stakeholder group reaching the Provost and the financial chair responsible for scholarship continuation decisions.
Tiered Program Design High-Stakes Stakeholder Management Program Cadence Design Cross-Functional Coordination Crisis-Aware Coaching
1
The Need
High-achieving scholarship students on probation, at risk of losing the funding that made enrollment possible

A population of competitive scholarship recipients had landed on academic probation, where failure didn't just mean poor grades but the potential loss of their ability to afford continued enrollment. The circumstances driving that probation varied widely: some students needed general academic skill support, while others were navigating crisis-level personal situations, including mental health struggles, family crisis, crime victimization, and housing instability. A one-size-fits-all probation response was insufficient for a population with that range of need.

2
The Learner
~250 scholarship students across two tiers of need

Tier 1 (approximately 150 students) primarily needed general study skills and academic habit support. Tier 2 (approximately 100 students) faced highly sensitive, often crisis-level circumstances underlying their academic struggles, requiring individualized, trauma-aware coaching beyond standard academic support. Both populations were managed within a single program structure rather than two separate programs.

3
The Approach
A fixed onboarding sequence, then a tiered coaching cadence, across a high-stakes stakeholder chain

Students attended weekly Skill Sessions for the first four weeks of the semester in a fixed sequence: Time Management, Study Skills, Procrastination, then Stress Management. After onboarding, students moved into monthly 1:1 coaching for the remainder of the term, with a three-session minimum. Many opted into additional weekly or biweekly sessions beyond the minimum. The two-tier design differentiated general academic support from crisis-aware individualized coaching within a single program structure. Stakeholder coordination spanned scholarship program directors, the Provost, and the financial chair responsible for scholarship continuation decisions.
